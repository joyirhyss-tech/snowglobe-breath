Hush

With Gratitude
to the Voices Held Here

A 60 second meditation app, closed each day with one of two hundred voices. This is a keepsake of who is held inside the quiet minute.

Kim Jo Yi · 김조이 · TPC | AEQ · 2026

Thank you.

Hush is a small thing. A user shakes their phone, glitter rises and settles inside a snowglobe, and at the end of one minute, one line appears on the dark, still water. That line is yours.

I built this so that the last thing a person hears at the close of their breath is a voice that has steadied me. Yours has steadied me. The library was researched against primary sources, and seven famous misattributions were lifted out so that the line under your name is, in fact, your line.

You are part of how someone returned to themselves today. I wanted you to know.

With love and respect,
Kim Jo Yi

Korean Buddhist teacher whose dharma talks bring everyday clarity to suffering, family, and conflict. He teaches that peace is a choice we make, again, today.

In Memoriam

Voices whose work continues without them. We close one minute a day with their words, and offer them this small bow of thanks.

Andrea Gibson

d. 2025 · genderqueer poet

Andrea wrote the open heart as a place of arrival. Their poems on love, gender, and survival made tenderness a kind of weather one chooses to live inside. We are warmer for them.

I will love you, with no need of an apology.

Audre Lorde

1934 to 1992 · Black lesbian feminist poet

Audre Lorde named self preservation as political warfare and gave generations permission to soften without apology. Her silences are the ones that taught us to speak. Her care is the inheritance.

There are so many silences to be broken.

Aunty Edith Kanakaʻole

1913 to 1979 · Native Hawaiian hula master

Aunty Edith carried the rhythm of Pele and the breath of the ʻāina in her chants. Her voice is the living voice of aloha ʻāina, and her teaching feeds those who would listen still.

ʻĀina is that which feeds.

Aunty Pilahi Paki

d. 1985 · Native Hawaiian cultural keeper of Aloha

Aunty Pilahi Paki taught aloha as a living covenant. Breath, presence, shared spirit. The Aloha Spirit in Hawaiʻi state law is her gift to those who would know what aloha truly means.

Aloha. The breath of life shared.

Baruch Spinoza

1632 to 1677 · Dutch Jewish philosopher

Spinoza taught that joy is the increase of our power to live, and that understanding is a form of love. His ethics is a slow, geometric devotion. Three centuries later, it still steadies the reader.

All things excellent are as difficult as they are rare.

bell hooks

1952 to 2021 · cultural critic and teacher

bell hooks insisted that love is a practice, disciplined and ordinary, and that this practice is how we end domination. Her writing made tenderness an intellectual stance. We are still learning from her.

Knowing how to be solitary is central to the art of loving.

Beopjeong Sunim (법정 스님)

1932 to 2010 · Korean Buddhist monk

Beopjeong Sunim made simplicity a path of joy. His essays on mu-soyu, non-possession, are a quiet hand on the shoulder of anyone carrying too much. He asked his books be allowed to go out of print, and so we hold them with extra care.

Non-possession does not mean having nothing. It means not having what is unnecessary.

Bhagavad Gita

c. 2nd century BCE to 2nd century CE · Sanskrit devotional dialogue

In the Gita, Krishna teaches Arjuna that action without attachment is a form of love. It has been a contemplative companion for two millennia, and it remains a steady voice for anyone who would set down the fruit and keep the work.

Set your heart upon your work, but never on its reward.

Black Elk (Heȟáka Sápa)

1863 to 1950 · Oglala Lakota holy man

Black Elk's visions opened a window into Lakota cosmology for a wider world. His witness teaches that the sacred hoop holds all peoples, and that everything done in the world is done in a circle.

Everything the Power of the World does is done in a circle.

Dhammapada

c. 3rd century BCE · Pali canon

The Dhammapada has guided practitioners for over two millennia. Its image rich teaching on mind, anger, and gentleness sits in the hand like a small lamp. The eternal rule is still the eternal rule.

Hatred does not cease by hatred, but only by love. This is the eternal rule.

Dōgen Zenji (道元禅師)

1200 to 1253 · Japanese Sōtō Zen founder

Dōgen taught that practice and enlightenment are not two. His Shōbōgenzō is the act of sitting itself, set into language. Eight centuries on, his sentences remain a doorway home.

Enlightenment is intimacy with all things.

Etel Adnan

1925 to 2021 · Lebanese-American poet and painter

Etel Adnan contemplated the mountain, the sea, and the slow weather of the soul. Her sentences were landscapes. To read her is to be looked at gently by a wise old friend.

To be is also to be in love.

Etty Hillesum

1914 to 1943 · Dutch Jewish diarist

Etty kept her diaries until her death at Auschwitz. She practiced beauty and presence in unimaginable circumstances and left a quiet vow that the inner life cannot be taken. Her well still holds water.

Despite everything, life is full of beauty and meaning.

Frederick Douglass

c. 1818 to 1895 · abolitionist orator and writer

Frederick Douglass made dignity, literacy, and freedom inseparable. He prayed with his legs and walked into history. His witness still steadies those who refuse to look away.

I prayed for freedom for twenty years, but received no answer until I prayed with my legs.

Gloria Anzaldúa

1942 to 2004 · Chicana lesbian theorist

Gloria Anzaldúa gave language to mestiza consciousness, a way of holding multiple worlds at once with tenderness. She made the borderland a sacred place. Wherever we go, we carry home on our backs because she said we could.

I change myself, I change the world.

Gwendolyn Brooks

1917 to 2000 · Pulitzer-winning Chicago poet

Gwendolyn Brooks wrote with respect for what is small and alive. Children, kitchens, hair. She made the South Side of Chicago the center of American poetry, and gave us the along to live in.

Live not for battles won. Live not for the end of the song. Live in the along.

Han Yongun (만해 한용운)

1879 to 1944 · Korean Buddhist monk and independence activist

Han Yongun bound love, freedom, and silence into devotional verse. His poems are stillness as resistance, and longing as a form of country. The beloved is gone, and yet the beloved keeps coming back.

Because I love, I leave you.

Haunani-Kay Trask

1949 to 2021 · Native Hawaiian scholar and poet

Haunani-Kay Trask returned Hawaiian sovereignty to the public conversation and wrote love for the land as a political force. Her chant for the ʻāina is still being sung.

From a Native daughter, a chant for the land.

Howard Thurman

1899 to 1981 · theologian and mystic

Howard Thurman mentored civil rights leaders in the practice of stillness. He believed the work of justice begins in the quiet listening of the inner life, and he gave generations the inward sea.

There is in every person an inward sea, and in that sea there is an island.

Ida B. Wells

1862 to 1931 · Chicago investigative journalist

Ida B. Wells held this country to its own claimed values. Her witness is steadied by the weight of what she would not look away from. We turn the light because she taught us how.

The way to right wrongs is to turn the light of truth upon them.

James Baldwin

1924 to 1987 · essayist and novelist

James Baldwin held love as a fierce, clear eyed witness against the lies of empire and the lies we tell ourselves. He taught that to love is to insist on the other person's full humanity. We are part of each other because he said so.

We are part of each other.

June Jordan

1936 to 2002 · poet, essayist, teacher

June Jordan insisted that love and Black life are political acts of imagination. She wrote with a fierce gentleness that taught readers to claim their own lives. We are the ones we have been waiting for, and she said it first.

We are the ones we have been waiting for.

Kabir

15th century · Indian mystic poet · trans. Tagore (1915)

Kabir refused caste and religious division and spoke of the divine within ordinary breath. His doha is a doorway: the garden you seek is in the body you already have.

In your body is the garden of flowers.

Kahlil Gibran

1883 to 1931 · Lebanese-American poet · The Prophet

Kahlil Gibran offered devotional, image rich counsel on love, work, and grief. His writing is a hand offered across the silence. A century later it is still a hand we accept.

Work is love made visible.

Kim Sowŏl (김소월)

1902 to 1934 · early twentieth century Korean poet

Kim Sowŏl made longing the music of the Korean language. His folk rooted lyrics are quiet doors held open. The azaleas of Mount Yaksan are still scattered on the path.

When you go away, weary of seeing me, I will let you go in silence, with care.

Kobayashi Issa (小林一茶)

1763 to 1828 · Japanese haiku poet · trans. Robert Hass

Issa held creatures, weather, and grief with humble warmth. His poems are small candles for the small lives. The snail still climbs Mount Fuji, slowly, slowly.

O snail, climb Mount Fuji, but slowly, slowly.

Langston Hughes

1901 to 1967 · Harlem Renaissance poet

Langston Hughes set Black daily life to the rhythm of blues and jazz, and made dreams a public, shared inheritance. His work returns the reader to the music of ordinary hope.

I, too, sing America.

Lao Tzu · Tao Te Ching

c. 4th century BCE · trans. Stephen Mitchell (1988)

The Tao Te Ching has shaped contemplative life for over two millennia. Its teaching on water, softness, and non doing is a quiet hand on the shoulder of the world. Nature does not hurry.

Nature does not hurry, yet everything is accomplished.

Lucille Clifton

1936 to 2010 · poet of survived hours

Lucille Clifton wrote toward joy as a daily, embodied vow. Her poems blessed the reader with ordinary, hard won life. She has shaped, into a kind of life, the way we celebrate ourselves.

Won't you celebrate with me what I have shaped into a kind of life?

Mahmoud Darwish

1941 to 2008 · Palestinian national poet

Mahmoud Darwish made longing for the homeland a universal devotional language. He wrote olives, mothers, and exile into the breath of poetry. We have on this earth what makes life worth living because he said so.

We have on this earth what makes life worth living.

Marsha P. Johnson

1945 to 1992 · Black trans Stonewall vanguard

Marsha gave love away as if she had infinite of it. She taught generations that joy is a form of revolt and that liberation is plural. The mother of Christopher Street walks with us still.

Darling, I want my gay rights now.

Mary Kawena Pukui · ʻŌlelo Noʻeau

1895 to 1986 · Bishop Museum Press

Mary Kawena Pukui compiled and translated 2,927 Hawaiian proverbs and poetical sayings, preserving the breath of the ancestors in the printed word. Hush holds seven of these. Each is a pebble of light.

Pūpūkahi i holomua. Unite to move forward.

Matsuo Bashō (松尾芭蕉)

1644 to 1694 · Japanese haiku master · trans. Robert Hass

Bashō refined haiku into a vessel for momentary, weather true presence. His verse is the breath of the season passing through. Every day is a journey, and the journey itself is home.

Every day is a journey, and the journey itself is home.

Maya Angelou

1928 to 2014 · poet and memoirist

Maya Angelou carried the dignity of the survivor and the laughter of someone who refused bitterness. She gave Black girlhood a public language of tenderness and rise. Still she rises, in all of us.

Still I rise.

N. Scott Momaday

1934 to 2024 · Kiowa novelist and poet

N. Scott Momaday taught that story is how a people remembers itself into being. His writing is an invitation to belong to a place. We are what we imagine, and his imagination is part of ours now.

We are what we imagine. Our very existence consists in our imagination of ourselves.

Octavia E. Butler

1947 to 2006 · speculative fiction visionary

Octavia Butler imagined survival as a practice of adaptation, kinship, and care. Her Earthseed parables wrote toward futures where Black women shape what comes next. Kindness eases change. So does she.

All that you touch you change. All that you change changes you.

Pablo Neruda

1904 to 1973 · Chilean Nobel laureate

Pablo Neruda wrote love and ordinary objects with cosmic tenderness. His odes return the reader to onions, socks, and silence with reverence. Rest with your dream in his dream.

And now you are mine. Rest with your dream in my dream.

Rabindranath Tagore

1861 to 1941 · Bengali Nobel laureate

Tagore set the natural world to song and the soul to a quiet, devotional listening. His poetry is a window left open. The butterfly counts not months but moments, and so does he.

The butterfly counts not months but moments, and has time enough.

Rumi (Jalāl ad-Dīn Muhammad Rūmī)

1207 to 1273 · Persian Sufi mystic · trans. Coleman Barks

Rumi's poetry of love, longing, and the open heart has been a companion to seekers for eight hundred years. His verses make the breath itself a prayer. The field is still there, beyond ideas.

Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing, there is a field. I'll meet you there.

Thich Nhat Hanh (Thầy)

1926 to 2022 · Vietnamese Zen teacher

Thầy brought engaged Buddhism to the world. His teaching on the present moment as a refuge has shaped contemplative practice for millions. His gift is the breath itself, and the smile that follows.

Smile, breathe, and go slowly.

Toni Morrison

1931 to 2019 · Nobel laureate

Toni Morrison held grief, memory, and Black interior life with a tenderness that taught readers to love themselves out loud. She believed beauty is something we make for one another, especially when the world refuses it. You are your best thing because she said it first.

You are your best thing.
